China’s Domestic and Import Pork Markets in COVID-19

Unsplash

Imported pork once threatened public safety

Recently in Tianjin, confirmed cases related to the Kan Hai Xuan community and Hai Lian cold storage had caused public panic. On November 24, 2020, Zhang Ying, the Deputy Director of Tianjin CDC, announced that they had found the Hai Lian Cold Storage’s infection origin at the press conference on the Prevention and Control of the Novel Coronavirus. The chief culprit is a pig head from North American.

It is not the first time that the outer packing being tested as positive for COV-19. The Tianjin case has deepened the public’s concern about imported frozen meat, particularly pork. In April, there was a severe COV-19 outbreak at Smithfield pork plant, the ninth-largest hog- processing facility in the US. Chinese consumers put lower confidence in imported frozen pork and tend to purchase local fresh meat. The government has reinforced safety control on imported pork at borders as more external packages tested positive on COVID-19. However, the ever-increasing demand for pork in China results in continuing imports, despite the risks involved.

The severe shortage of domestic pork supply

China is the world’s largest pork producer and consumer. The average Chinese person could consume 30kg of pork a year. In 2019, the spread of African swine fever hit hard on domestic production in China. The ASF led to the culling of more than 1.1 million pigs out of an estimated herd of 350 million across the country.

Meanwhile, the Chinese government introduced more stringent environmental protection policies in the pig industry in 2019, forcing out a large number of small and medium pig farms classified as “dirty, messy, bad” and “substandard disposal”. The number of marketed pigs dropped sharply to 540 million heads, resulting in a year-on-year decrease of 21.57%.


In 2019, Pork prices in China once hit the historical record level with 30 yuan (US$4.36) per kilogram. There was ridicule that anyone that could afford pork must be a millionaire. To fill the pork market’s vacancy, China has to import a large amount of foreign pork and meet the domestic demand. Starting from January 1. 2020, the country has lowered the import tariff on the frozen pork from 12% to 8% temporarily, helping to increase pork imports and ease the shortage of local supply.

Rapid growth in China’s pork imports market

The year-to-year pork import growth rate presents a stunning upward-trend since 2019, with the highest growth rate of 190.4% in imports quantity, equivalent to 412.9% in total value compared with the same period last year. From March 2019 to 2020, the single-month pork import volume has increased by more than 100% for seven times.


According to Huajing Industry Research Institution, the pork imports have an impressive growth with a 170% increase in quantity, and 386.4% of total imports account from January to March 2020. The institution showed that the pork imports’ average price is 30827.27 thousand dollars per 10 thousand tons, equivalent to 3.08 dollars per kilogram.


During the Spring Festival, Chinese families consume lots of pork to make bacon and prepare for New Year’s Eve. Hence the live slaughter pig prices tended to grow, following a drop after the festival. Driving by a massive demand for pork supply, China’s pork imports market had a rapid development starting from January 2020.


Qianzhan Industry Research Institute released an exclusive analysis on the Status Quo and Development Trend of China’s Pork Import and Export Market, showing that Brazil, Europe, and the US were the prime beneficiaries of the explosive increase in China’s pork supply. The pork imports from the European Union increased by 76% in 2019.


Chinese government measures on the domestic pork industry

To boost pork production, the government encourages the hog farmers to enlarge the scale of the hoggery. There are 728 newly-built farms in large-scale put into production in October. Since the beginning of 2020, the cumulative number of large farms in construction has reached 13,000, with another 15,000 large farms re-opened and put into use.


The two terms “pig” and “hog” are used distinctly in this industry. In America and most of the world, a swine is classified as a pig if it weighs less than 50 kilograms. And it is a hog if it weighs greater than 50 kilograms. As for commercial usage, pigs are the farming swine that are not ready to be slaughtered, and hogs are the swine that is ready to be slaughtered.

An economic phenomenon known as the pork cycle describes cyclical fluctuations in supply and prices in livestock markets. In short, more investments are made when prices are high; however, the investments’ effect is delayed because of long breeding time (the production lag), forming a repetitive cycle. Because of the pork cycle effect, farms need to proactively consider the prices and change the breeding plan from time to time.

Besides encourage local farming development, the government actively imported superior swine genetics from companies worldwide. Since imports resumed in early 2020, a number of breeding pigs had been flown into China to ease the supply shortage in domestic farms. Three planeloads of breeding pigs, including Duroc, Landrace, and Yorkshire from Genesus UK herds arrived at Xuzhou airport, Jiangsu. Before the journey, the imported pigs have been transferred to the quarantine facility of Scandinavian Farms Pig Industries Limited for inspection.


“High prices hurt the people, low prices hurt the farmers.”, a two-part allegorical saying sequel reflects the effect of pork prices and the market. In the constantly changing environment under COVID-19, China’s Domestic and Import Pork Markets are facing unprecedented opportunities and challenges.
